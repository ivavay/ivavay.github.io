Fuse

An app that connects people together for networking opportunities at events

Timeline: July 2019 - Ongoing
Role: User Research, Low-Fidelity, High-Fidelity, Branding
Partner: Ananya

Introduction

In June of 2019, my friend and I stumbled our way into SF Design Week and crashed some of their networking events. We’ve found that it was incredibly hard to find people to talk to at these events because 1.) people are usually already chatting away in their formed circles 2.) it was hard to find people who are working at the company who is hosting the event 3.) when we found those people, they might not even be interested in talking to us students because of reasons still yet to be investigated 4.) music is usually too loud for a proper conversation to even occur. Having come all the way from hours away to attend these events, it was frustrating for us to not walk away with the optimal experience. We were then curious and convinced that many also experience these problems in connecting with others at events, so we thought that it’d be fun to work on an event-based networking app to help people walk away from these events with continuable relationships.

User Research

After conducting user interviews on four people, I found some themes/patterns that were common across the people that I talked to, as well as some quotes that jumped out at me:
- Desire to learn about career pathways, such as how a person they admire got to where they are today
- Prefer mentor/mentee programs where they could get resources for design & general advice
- Advice given in the form of actionable things to work towards is incredibly helpful

Quotes

“Don’t do well in that type of situation - it’s just not for me - I feel like a lot of it is just small talk and not going really deep into things “
“Also should be a two way street!”
“ I think that’s why Ed Chao’s advice stuck really well, because it had areas to get good at. Now though I need help to get better at visual design and prototyping and probably product thinking, so I’d look for mentorship in those areas. “

I then came up with this how-might-we statement to define the problem. This then became the compass in driving my initial paper sketches.

How might we provide an event experience where people could form mentor-mentee relationships that don’t feel transactional?/span>
